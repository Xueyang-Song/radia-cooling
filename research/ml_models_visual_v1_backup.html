Chapter 0

What Is Machine Learning?

Teaching computers to recognize patterns in data so they can make predictions — without being explicitly programmed for every case.

The Core Idea

Input = features you feed to the model (particle size, layer thickness…)
Output = prediction (solar reflectance, cooling power…)
Training = showing thousands of examples so the model learns the pattern
Inference = using the trained model on new, unseen inputs

Click to retrain
Imagine tasting 1,000 coffees, recording beans, roast time, water temp, and grind size. Eventually you'd feel which combos make great coffee — even recipes you've never tried. ML does that with math.

Three Learning Paradigms

Supervised

Learns from labeled examples (input → known output). Used for predicting cooling performance.

Unsupervised

Finds hidden patterns without labels. Not a main focus of this paper.

Generative

Creates new data samples. Used for inverse design — generating material structures from target performance.

Traditional ML

K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

The simplest ML algorithm. Find the K most similar examples you already know and average their answers.

Traditional ML

How It Works

When a new data point comes in, KNN measures the "distance" to every known example, picks the K closest, and averages their outputs. No training phase — it just memorizes the data.

Click to classify a new point
You move to a new neighborhood and want to guess your house price. Look at the 5 most similar houses nearby and average their prices. That's KNN.

Strengths

  • Dead simple to understand & implement
  • No training phase — just memorize data
  • Works well when relationships are smooth

Weaknesses

  • Slow with large datasets
  • Struggles with many input variables
  • Doesn't learn abstract patterns
Used to predict optimal structure parameters and absorption/emission performance of metasurface daytime radiative coolers. Works best when design variables are few and well-defined.
Traditional ML

Random Forest

A committee of many decision trees that each vote on the answer. Wisdom of the crowd.

Traditional ML

How It Works

A single decision tree is a flowchart of yes/no questions. A Random Forest builds hundreds of trees, each on a random subset, then averages their votes. The averaging smooths out individual quirks.

Watch the forest vote
Ask 100 friends to each guess tomorrow's temperature, but each friend only sees 70% of the weather data. Their average is usually better than any single guess.

Strengths

  • Handles non-linear relationships
  • Tells you which features matter most
  • Resistant to overfitting

Weaknesses

  • Can be slow with huge datasets
  • Less effective for very complex patterns
  • Bad at extrapolation
Used for predicting absorption/emission of metasurface radiative coolers and scattering efficiency of particles in passive RC systems. Outperformed other traditional models.
Traditional ML

XGBoost

Builds trees sequentially — each new tree corrects the mistakes of all previous trees. The king of structured data.

Traditional ML

How It Works

Unlike Random Forest (parallel trees), XGBoost builds trees one at a time. Each new tree specifically targets the errors left by all prior trees. The final prediction is the sum of all trees' contributions.

Watch error correction in action
Take an exam, review only the questions you got wrong, study just those topics, retake. XGBoost does this hundreds of times.

Strengths

  • Often best-performing on tabular data
  • Very fast (parallelized)
  • Feature importance / SHAP explainability

Weaknesses

  • More hyperparameters to tune
  • Can overfit on very small datasets
  • Not ideal for raw spectral curves
Predicted thermal properties of RC aerogels with higher accuracy than other models. Combined with SHAP to identify which factors most influence cooling performance — a dual predictor + explainer role.
Neural Networks

Artificial Neural Network (ANN)

Layers of artificial neurons that transform input through learned mathematical operations. The foundation of deep learning.

Neural Network

How It Works

Data flows from input → hidden layers → output. Each neuron multiplies inputs by learned weights, sums them, and applies a non-linear activation function. During training, backpropagation adjusts all weights to reduce error.

Watch data flow through the network
An assembly line where each worker takes the previous worker's product, modifies it slightly, and passes it forward. After thousands of practice runs, the line produces exactly what's needed.

Strengths

  • Learns complex non-linear relationships
  • Great surrogate for physics simulations
  • Handles multiple inputs & outputs

Weaknesses

  • Needs more data than traditional ML
  • "Black box" — hard to interpret
  • Must tune architecture carefully
Predicts long-term RC performance of windows under different climates. Used as a fundamental surrogate model replacing expensive EM simulations (FDTD, TMM).
Neural Networks

Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)

Slides a small window across data to detect local patterns — perfect for spectral data where peaks, dips, and plateaus matter.

Neural Network

How It Works

A spectrum is a 1D sequence. CNN slides small filters (kernels) across it to detect features — peaks, dips, edges. Multiple layers combine basic features into complex patterns like "high solar reflectance + atmospheric window emission."

Watch the filter slide across the spectrum
When you read text, your eyes slide across groups of letters to recognize words. CNN does the same with spectral data, recognizing "spectral words."

Strengths

  • Excellent at local pattern detection
  • Parameter-efficient (shared filters)
  • Automatically learns important features

Weaknesses

  • Needs more training data
  • Less interpretable
  • Architecture requires expertise
Maps complex spectral requirements to structural parameters, especially for inverse design. Automatically extracts meaningful features from high-dimensional spectral curves.
Neural Networks

Tandem Neural Network

Chains a forward + inverse network to solve the one-to-many problem in inverse design.

Neural Network

The One-to-Many Problem

Forward: one structure → one spectrum (unique). Inverse: one target spectrum → MANY possible structures (ambiguous!). Direct inverse training fails because contradictory examples confuse the network.

The Tandem Solution

Train a forward net first, freeze it, then chain an inverse net before it. The inverse net proposes a structure, and the frozen forward net checks if it produces the target spectrum. The loss is measured at the spectral output — the inverse net just needs to find any structure that works.

Inverse → Forward → Compare
Instead of asking "what recipe makes this exact cake?" (hard, many recipes), you ask "find me a recipe, and I'll check if the cake tastes right" (easier, just optimize taste).
Used for colored daytime radiative coolers where both color fidelity and cooling performance must be satisfied simultaneously. Handles the inherent ambiguity of spectrum → structure mapping.
Optimization

Optimization Algorithms

Search algorithms that find the best design parameters. Combined with ML surrogates: the ML model predicts fast, the optimizer searches smart.

Optimizer

Genetic Algorithm (GA)

Mimics biological evolution: a population of designs evolves via selection, crossover, and mutation. The fittest survive and reproduce.

Watch evolution in action
Breeding dogs — pick the fastest, breed them together, occasional random mutations. After many generations: championship greyhounds.

Key strength: Naturally handles mixed discrete + continuous variables. Choosing from {SiO₂, TiO₂, Al₂O₃} AND optimizing thickness? GA does both.

NSGA-II variant: Multi-objective — finds a Pareto front (trade-off curve) for solar reflectance vs. IR emission vs. aesthetics.

The most commonly used optimizer for multilayer film design. Optimizes material type, layer count, thickness, and ordering in RC structures.
Optimizer

Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)

Simulates a flock of birds. Each "particle" (candidate design) has position and velocity, attracted toward its own best find and the swarm's best find.

Watch the swarm converge
A flock of birds looking for a lake. Each remembers the wettest area it found AND knows the wettest area any bird found. They drift toward good areas while still exploring.

Best for: Continuous parameters — particle size, film thickness, period, fill fraction.

Optimizes microstructure geometry and particle-based systems. Highlighted workflow: FDTD → Neural network surrogate → Adaptive PSO, achieving R² = 0.99841.
Optimizer

Bayesian Optimization (BO)

The most sample-efficient optimizer. Designed for when each evaluation is very expensive. Builds a probabilistic model that tracks both expected performance AND uncertainty, then picks the single best next point to try.

Explore vs Exploit
Looking for the best restaurant in a city. Try 5 random ones, then each time ask: "Given what I know, which restaurant would teach me the most?" You find the best in ~20 tries instead of 500.
Guo et al. (2020) combined RCWA + BO and found optimal selective-emission structures using <1% of the candidate space. Specifically highlighted for colored RC trade-off optimization.
Generative Models

Variational Autoencoder (VAE)

Compresses designs into a compact code, then reconstructs. New designs are generated by sampling from the organized latent space.

Generative

How It Works

Encoder: compresses a design into a small latent code (e.g., 10 numbers).
Decoder: reconstructs a design from the code.
Generation: sample a new point in latent space → decode → brand new design!

Encode → Latent Space → Decode
A fashion designer who studies thousands of dresses, learns the "essence" of what makes a dress, then sketches new dresses by tweaking that essence.

Strengths

  • Diverse new designs, not just the "single best"
  • Smooth latent space — interpolation works
  • Provides uncertainty via probabilistic formulation

Weaknesses

  • Outputs can be "blurry" (tend toward average)
  • Quality usually lower than GANs
  • Tricky loss balancing during training
Key generative approach for RC inverse design, enabling exploration of new designs not limited to the training set.
Generative Models

Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)

Two networks compete: a Generator creates fake designs, a Discriminator detects fakes. The competition drives ever-better generation.

Generative

How It Works

Generator: takes random noise + target spec, produces a design.
Discriminator: judges "real simulation data" vs. "Generator's fake."
They train together in a minimax game until fakes are indistinguishable from real data.

Generator vs Discriminator
An art forger (Generator) vs. an art detective (Discriminator). The forger keeps improving until even experts can't tell forgeries from genuine paintings.

Strengths

  • Very high-quality, realistic designs
  • Handles complex multi-modal spaces
  • Sharper outputs than VAEs

Weaknesses

  • Training is notoriously unstable
  • Mode collapse risk
  • No uncertainty estimates
Highlighted as a key frontier for inverse design. A conditional GAN can accept "95% solar reflectance, 90% emissivity in 8-13 μm, looks blue" and generate structures that meet all targets.
Generative Models

Diffusion Models

Learn to reverse a destruction process: gradually add noise until data becomes static, then train a network to denoise step by step.

Generative

How It Works

Forward: gradually add noise to a real design → pure random static.
Reverse: starting from static, a learned denoiser removes noise step by step → clean design emerges.

Noise → Denoise → Design

Strengths

  • State-of-the-art generation quality
  • More stable training than GANs
  • Naturally handles diverse solutions

Weaknesses

  • Slow generation (many denoising steps)
  • Requires very large datasets
  • Computationally expensive
Emerging frontier alongside GANs/VAEs. Not yet widely applied in RC but already shows promise in nanophotonics and materials science.
Generative Models

Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL)

Treats design as a game: an agent takes sequential actions (add a layer, pick a material) and gets rewards for how close it gets to target performance.

Generative

How It Works

State: current partial design (e.g., 3 layers placed).
Action: add SiO₂ at 150 nm.
Reward: improvement in cooling performance.
Through thousands of "games," the agent learns an optimal design strategy.

Agent learns layer by layer
Training a dog with treats. The agent (dog) takes actions (tricks) and gets rewards (treats) for good performance. Gradually learns the best strategy.
Yu et al. used DQN for emissivity engineering. Also cited: RL-based inverse design of composite films for spacecraft thermal control. Promising for sequential design decisions.
The Big Picture

The Three-Stage Pipeline

Models aren't used in isolation — they form a progression that the field is moving through.

Stage 1: Forward Prediction

"Given this structure, what's its performance?"

  • KNN
  • Random Forest
  • XGBoost
  • ANN / CNN
→

Stage 2: Optimization

"What's the best design in this space?"

  • GA (NSGA-II)
  • PSO
  • Bayesian Opt
  • + Stage 1 surrogate
→

Stage 3: Inverse/Generative

"Given target performance, generate a structure."

  • Tandem NN
  • VAE / GAN
  • Diffusion Models
  • DRL / DQN
